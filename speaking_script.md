# Split Second — 2-minute speaking script

*~300 words. Natural pace ≈ 1:55–2:05. No intro.*

---

Split Second is a branching emergency response simulation that puts the learner in the driver's seat of a first responder arriving at a highway rollover. It's built to train judgment under pressure — not recall, not procedure memorization, but the kind of thinking that decides whether one emergency becomes two.

*(brief pause)*

The scenario opens on a night dispatch call. A sedan has rolled onto its side against the guardrail on eastbound Highway 30. One trapped driver. Visible smoke from the engine bay. A fuel leak pooling on the asphalt. Traffic is still creeping past, and bystanders are already reaching through broken glass trying to help.

That's where the learner takes over. You are first on scene. You have seconds to make a call. And the simulation gives you exactly two choices.

*(slow down slightly)*

Option A is the one that feels heroic — extract the driver immediately. Rush in, pull them out, trust that speed beats the hazard. Option B slows the tempo down. You block traffic, move bystanders back, request suppression coverage, and run a coordinated extrication.

The video outcomes show what each choice actually produces. Branch A turns into a second emergency — the engine bay flares, the team loses coordination, and now you've got a medical case and an active ignition threat. Branch B is slower, but every hazard gets controlled before anyone touches the vehicle, and the driver comes out clean.

*(land the closing calmly)*

The point isn't that rushing is always wrong. The point is that scene instability doesn't care about your intentions. This tool gives responders repetitions on that exact moment of choice — safely, visually, in under three minutes — so when the real call comes in, the instinct is already trained.
